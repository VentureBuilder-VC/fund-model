How To Use This Model
A complete walkthrough of every tab, every input, and every number — so you can build your fund thesis with confidence

Venture Builder Fund I — Dynamic Model

This model simulates a closed-end fund (CEF) that combines three income engines — a core income portfolio, private credit with warrants, and a venture builder equity programme — into a single 12-year vehicle targeting a 7% fixed annual yield to LPs. Every number is live: change any input and every tab recalculates instantly. This guide explains what each tab does, what every input means, and how the math connects.

Start Here — Recommended Workflow

Follow this order when exploring or stress-testing the model:

  1. Fund Inputs — set your fund size, yield target, fee structure, and allocation splits. Everything flows from here.
  2. Exit Scenarios — review and adjust the equity outcome buckets and year-by-year exit timing weights. These drive NAV shape and cap gain distributions.
  3. Dashboard — read the headline KPIs to confirm the fund makes economic sense at your inputs.
  4. Annual P&L — verify year-by-year income, NII, coverage, and NAV path. Check that NAV stays above $125M.
  5. Yield & Distributions — confirm coverage ratios hold and run the Investor Scenario tool to show LP outcomes at different entry prices.
  6. CEF Metrics — check the premium/discount curve, dividend yield vs share price, and NAV per share trajectory.
  7. Fee Waterfall — verify GP economics: management fee, performance fee, and total GP take over fund life.
  8. Term Strategy — model the liquidation: what IRR do LPs achieve at different exit prices and discount rates?
  9. Audit 🔍 — run the integrity check. Every assumption is benchmarked against real-world CEF/BDC standards.
  10. Fund Analysis 📈 — generate an AI-powered institutional memo from your live model data.

LEVERAGE  Tactical Bridge & Structural Leverage

Short-term leverage in years 1–2 only, drawn on the PC book at a higher lending rate. Creates an income boost while the equity programme is ramping. Default 20% of fund size ($25M at 13.5%/4.5%).
Structural Leverage
Ongoing leverage (optional). BDC regulation permits up to 2× assets; this model defaults to 0% (unlevered post-TAC).

EXIT TIMING  Year-by-Year Weights

The exit weight table controls when proceeds are realised across the 12-year fund life. Weights must sum to 100%. The default schedule peaks in Years 9–10 — reflecting a 2-year rollout + 7-year average hold period before exits.

capGainDiv (yr N) = totalEqProceeds × exitWeight[N] × (1 − perfFee%)
Exit proceeds paid to LPs reduce fund NAV by the same amount (cash leaves the fund)
⚠️ Front-loading exits (peak in years 6–8) causes NAV to dip below $125M because exit distributions outpace equity appreciation. The default back-loaded schedule keeps NAV above $125M throughout. You can override weights in the table but check the Audit tab after.

WARRANTS  Attached to Private Credit Loans

Every private credit loan carries a warrant to purchase equity in the borrowing company at the loan origination value. Warrants exercise when loans mature (~24 months) or on exit events. They are independent from the equity programme and modelled with their own outcome buckets and timing weights.

Warrant Coverage
% of loan face value received as an equity stake at strike. Default 15%.
Strike Price
Valuation at which the warrant can be exercised. Default $2M per company.

How NAV Is Calculated

NAV BRIDGE  Year-End Formula

NAV(End) = NAV(Start)
  + Equity Appreciation  // appraiser mark-up of portfolio at valApprRate%
  + New Company Value Added  // new companies joined this year, valued at $300K each
  − New Company Acquisition Cost  // $50K onboarding cost per new company
  − Fee Draw from NAV  // only if NII < 0 (all income exhausted) — rare
  − Capital Gain Distributions  // exit proceeds paid to LPs leave the fund

Note: Base distributions (7% yield) and surplus distributions are paid from income, not from NAV. They do not reduce NAV. Only capital gain distributions (exit proceeds) reduce NAV because cash physically leaves the fund.

Investor's pro-rata share of the fund's final NAV at liquidation.

Simple annualised return treating all payments as received at term end.

Tips & Common Questions

Why does Year 1 show amber coverage?

No equity or warrant exits occur in Year 1. NII alone covers ~92% of the 7% yield. The tactical leverage bridge helps close the gap. Coverage reaches 100%+ from Year 2 onwards as warrant proceeds start flowing. This is normal and expected — amber in Year 1 does not mean the fund is undercovering.

Why does NAV grow above $125M even though we pay a 7% yield?

The 7% yield is paid from income (NII), not from NAV. NAV grows because: (1) the equity portfolio appreciates at the independent appraiser's rate each year, and (2) new portfolio companies are added at a value-above-cost uplift in years 1–3. Exit distributions reduce NAV only by the carry taken by the GP — the capital return and LP profit share flow out to investors while appreciation on the remaining portfolio more than offsets it. In the base case shown, NAV may remain above Day‑1, but NAV can be volatile and can decline under downside scenarios (see Risk tab).

What's the difference between CAGR and IRR in the yield scenarios?

CAGR treats all distributions as if received at the end of the fund term — ignoring the time value of annual payments. IRR gives credit for receiving distributions early (Year 2 money is worth more than Year 12 money). For an income fund paying 7% annually, IRR will always be higher than CAGR. The IRR is the correct metric for comparing LP returns to other investments.

Does the default rate reduce NAV?

It depends on accounting policy. By default the model carries the private credit book at par (BDC-style), so defaults reduce income and coverage but do not automatically write down NAV. If you enable Mark credit impairments to NAV in the Risk tab, the model applies a Loss-Given-Default (LGD) assumption and records principal impairments as NAV write-downs.

Why is equity appreciation shown in the P&L table but not in Gross Income?

Equity appreciation is a NAV item, not an income item. The appraiser's annual mark-up of the portfolio increases NAV but cannot be distributed as cash until assets are sold. It is shown in the P&L table for reference (column labelled Eq. Appr. ↑NAV) but is deliberately excluded from Gross Income. Only realised exit proceeds flow into Gross Income.

🤖 Using the AI Widget When Hosting Externally

Why it works in Claude.ai but not on your own server

The AI widget calls the Anthropic API directly from the browser. Claude.ai handles authentication transparently. When you host the file yourself (e.g. on GitHub Pages, Netlify, your own server), browsers block direct API calls due to CORS policy — you need a small backend proxy that holds your API key securely.

      headers: { 'Content-Type': 'application/json', 'x-api-key': env.ANTHROPIC_KEY, 'anthropic-version': '2023-06-01' },
      body
    });
    const data = await res.text();
    return new Response(data, { headers: { ...corsHeaders(), 'Content-Type': 'application/json' } });
  }
};
function corsHeaders() {
  return { 'Access-Control-Allow-Origin': '*', 'Access-Control-Allow-Methods': 'POST,OPTIONS', 'Access-Control-Allow-Headers': 'Content-Type' };
}
